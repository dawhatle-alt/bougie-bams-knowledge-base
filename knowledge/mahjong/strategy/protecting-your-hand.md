# Protecting Your Hand

## Summary

Strong players guard information as carefully as tiles: they delay exposures until the hand is well developed, keep their rack arrangement unreadable, and avoid physical and verbal tells that reveal what they need.

## Details

Delay exposures: every exposure narrows what your hand can be and surrenders flexibility. A useful habit is waiting until roughly seven or eight tiles of the target hand are in place before making a first call, unless the call is clearly decisive.

Manage your rack: experienced opponents read racks. Gaps between tile groups, flipped blocks, and left-to-right structure all leak information. Keep spacing uniform and place new exposures to the left of the rack so the hand's internal order stays hidden.

Control tells: sighing at a discard, hesitating before passing, or staring at one end of the rack telegraphs your position. Aim for the same tempo and demeanor whether a discard helps you or not.

Keep flowers early: flowers appear in many hands on a typical card, so passing them early in the Charleston is usually a gift to an opponent.

Have an exit plan: pair this discipline with a pivot hand that shares tiles with your primary target, and switch to pure defense when your hand dies rather than feeding live discards.

## Examples

- A player waits to call a pung because the hand is only five tiles developed, keeping a concealed option alive.
- A player rearranges their rack to even spacing after an opponent glances at it repeatedly.

## Common Mistakes

- Making a quick early exposure that reveals the hand family and forfeits the concealed bonus.
- Leaving visible gaps between groups on the rack, effectively publishing the hand's structure.
